The sun has barely risen on a below freezing, cloudless January day when research scientist Jessie Reese and the researchers from Bird Conservancy of the Rockies pull off onto a dirt track in the Chihuahuan Desert.  

It’s their ninth consecutive day of searching for four grassland bird species facing significant population declines.

The team of biologists followed these tiny songbirds to their New Mexico wintering grounds in order to study the threats they are facing, as over the half century, each species has lost between 60 to 70 percent of their populations.

As they near their worksite, Reese and avian ecologists, Nick Haddad and Kylie Lamoree, poke their heads out of the truck windows, carefully scanning the golden and dusty-green grasses for chestnut-collared longspurs, horned larks, grasshopper sparrows and baird's sparrows. [[ImageEmbed src="/images/polaroid_1.png" size="inline-right" alt="7:30 a.m., Reese looking out of the truck window for longspurs and sparrows."]]

Reese started out studying birds in a similar fashion — with her head out of a car window, more than 15 years ago.

She became interested in researching birds after taking an ornithology course in college where her professor would load students up into a 12-passenger van at 7 a.m. and drive around the county looking at birds.

"My eyes were open to an entire world that I had no idea existed," Reese said.

But that world is currently threatened as across North America, grasslands and the birds that depend on them are rapidly vanishing, data shows.

Despite their unassuming appearance — often distinguished by muted colors such as brown, white or gray to help them blend in with the landscape — these ground-dwelling birds are vital to the health and sustainability of the Great Plains, providing essential ecological services such as pest control, seed dispersal, nutrient cycling and pollination.

More than half of the bird species dependent on grasslands for breeding are in steep decline. And despite ongoing efforts by conservationists and landowners across the Great Plains to restore habitat and curb losses, the declines are accelerating, according to a recent study in the journal Science. Climate change and cuts to laws and governmental funding that protect birds and their habitats has exacerbated the downward trend.  

The decrease in birds coincides with a dramatic change in open grasslands, which is being converted into farmland at a rate of roughly 620,000 acres per year, according to a Berkeley Journalism analysis of the U.S. Geological Survey National Land Cover Change data. As a result, over 24 million acres have been lost in the last four decades, which is roughly equivalent to the size of Indiana. The collapse of the birds and grasslands could result in a modern day Dust Bowl, which scientists say would cause major disruptions in the U.S. food supply and economy.  

Scientists hope their research coupled with new programs will curb the losses and result in a rebound in some of the birds lost, because anything else could be catastrophic.

“Grasslands are the lifeblood of the central part of the U.S. and if they disappear, then this landscape will be drastically altered,” Brandt Ryder, chief conservation scientist at Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, said.

Reese, who has led Bird Conservancy’s Migratory Ecology Program since January 2025, is one of many conservation professionals racing to figure out how to stall this looming extinction crisis.

Started in 2020, the ecology program aims to grow understanding of grassland bird migration across the Great Plains, but especially in the Chihuahuan Desert, which has been relatively understudied.  [[ImageEmbed src="/illustrations/chihuahuan_desert.png" size="margin-right" alt="Chihuahuan Desert map"]]

“A lot of the birds we're studying breed in the Northern Great Plains and spend the winter here in the Chihuahuan Desert. So understanding if birds that winter here in southern New Mexico are going to Montana or Alberta or Wyoming — that allows us to understand what pressures those birds are facing across different population segments,” Reese said. “And it allows us to understand if things that are happening down here in New Mexico are having an undue influence on any one breeding population and vice versa.”

For the past three winters, field crews from Bird Conservancy have traveled to New Mexico to tag and monitor grassland birds on their non-breeding grounds using Motus technology.

The Motus Wildlife Tracking System, started in 2014 by Birds Canada, is a global collaborative network of automated telemetry stations that allows researchers to study the movements of small animals tagged with radio transmitters. Prior to 2020, there was a significant lack of Motus stations in the Great Plains and longstanding research on grassland birds had mostly been conducted on the breeding grounds.

“Bird Conservancy has really helped the Motus Network grow in the Great Plains and the Chihuahuan Desert,” Reese said. In the past five years, the organization has installed over 100 radio towers and tagged almost 400 grassland birds.

Method 1: Flush netting

After about an hour of birding, Reese and the research team begin preparing their equipment. They start by setting up a row of seven consecutive segments of mist nets attached to tall metal poles. The segments vary between six, nine and 12 meters long.

The location of the nets was chosen based on where the crew suspects grasshopper sparrows and baird's sparrows to be hiding in the brush. Once set up is complete, the group heads back to the cars to strategize their approach to capture birds.

The first method they used is known as flush netting.

In a line formation the crew walks towards the nets, dragging a long rope with milk jugs attached and swiping the grass with long sticks to flush the birds up from the ground.

As they start zeroing in on the nets, avian field technician Nick Schleissman sees a bird in reach. He shouts “Bird!” to alert the rest of the crew. They pause for a moment to adjust their position before taking off in a full sprint to force the bird into the net.  

Through the flush netting method, the researchers captured four savannah sparrows. After each catch, they carefully untangle the birds from nets, place them in a soft cloth bag.

The savannah sparrow is not one of their target species, so crew leader Sophie Barno makes a quick record of the catches before Reese releases each bird.

*******

A biome in collapse

Despite being among the most endangered ecosystems in the U.S., grasslands are the least protected, according to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service.

“Most people think of grasslands as a wasteland. They think if we need to protect something, we should be protecting the rainforest,” Ryder said. “But grasslands probably are more important in many ways than the rainforest for many reasons. And so folks are not tuned into that. They also don't recognize that that's where their food comes from.”

Sixty-two percent of the North American grassland biome has been lost due to agricultural conversion, woody-plant invasion, drought and other climate change-related stressors.

Continued loss of this land would reduce carbon sequestration, diminish pollinator habitat and reduce soil quality and moisture retention. This has lasting implications not only for birds, but also for the U.S. food supply, as worsening environmental conditions impact crop yields.

As climate change triggers rising temperatures, shifts in precipitation patterns and more frequent extreme weather events such as drought and wildfires, the loss of healthy grasslands for birds and agriculture falls into an intensifying spiral.

“Grasslands are essential for the economic livelihoods of millions of people across this geography and so we have all these reasons why we need to protect and restore them,” Ryder said.

Contributing to the collapse is the country’s need for crops and beef. Roughly quarter of all U.S. crops and half of the country’s beef cattle are grown in the Great Plains, creating a tension between birds and agriculture.

In the Northern Great Plains states, roughly 1.2 million acres of grasslands have been converted to farmland since 2020, according to a Berkeley Journalism analysis of the USGS National Land Cover Change data.

One of the places Bird Conservancy determined as a high conservation priority through its models created using data from Motus and various other sources is the Prairie Pothole Region of the Northern Great Plains.

The Prairie Pothole Region consists of a mixture of depressional wetlands and prairie habitat that are used as breeding grounds for up to 50 percent of North America’s duck and waterfowl populations, as well as several grassland species.

Of all bird types, water birds have managed to increase their populations. It has given scientists some hope.

However, in recent years, their numbers have also been in decline due to widespread drought in prairie potholes. Experts hope the numbers will rebound like they have historically, but they worry that if they don't, it could also mean that grassland birds will suffer even more.

Data shows that some grassland birds — including the chestnut-collared longspur, baird's sparrow, lark bunting, thick-billed longspur and sprague's pipit — are declining nearly twice as fast in the Prairie Pothole Region as compared to the rest of the United States.

In the Southern and Central Great Plain states, another threat driving the loss of grasslands includes the encroachment of woody plants, such as red cedar and mesquite trees.

Throughout history, indigenous land practices and free roaming bison helped to maintain the grassland biome. However, after decades of fire suppression, the near elimination of bison on the land, as well as the creation of windbreaks for agricultural fields, the trees have grown rampant.

“The reality is that we can't restore all of the grassland that's been lost in North America,” Reese said. “So we really need to understand where we should be doing conservation work — are there places that are going to be more beneficial where we'll get a bigger bang for our conservation buck?”

*******

Leveraging Bird Behavior

After two rounds of flush netting, the Bird Conservancy crew was unable to capture any of their target species, so they broke down the nets and set up in a new spot in hopes of better results.

This time, the goal is to catch the chestnut-collared longspurs — relying on the birds’ own behavior to lure them into the nets. They set up around a cattle pond where the longspurs flock around to drink.

With the nets surrounding the pond, the crew quietly watches from a distance with their binoculars, waiting for the birds to fly into the net. As the flock inches closer, each researchers’ head synchronously pops up on alert, like a group of meerkats peeking out of its den.

Then it happens — they spot a chestnut-collared longspur fly into the net. Haddad takes off running untangle it while the others get the banding and radio equipment ready.

While LaMoore and Haddad kept watch of the nets for more catches, Reese and the monitoring technicians recorded data on each bird’s age, sex, weight, muscle and fat content, wings, tail and feathers.

This chestnut-collared longspur is a young adult male, which the team determined by examining the feather colors and development, as fully grown males have distinct yellow throats, rusty napes and stark white tail feathers.  

Once data on its physical measurements was collected, the technicians attached a Motus tag that sits like a tiny, lightweight backpack between the bird’s wings.

Ready for monitoring, Reese safely released the chestnut-collared longspur back into its habitat.

The same process, which takes about 20 minutes, is repeated when the crew catches a horned lark shortly after.

With tags attached, as these birds move across North America, Bird Conservancy can track any time they fly within 10 to 15 kilometers of a Motus station. This data will help the organization to better understand their migratory life cycles and determine where to focus conservation efforts.

*******

Conservation work under attack

After reassuming office last year, President Donald Trump and his administration pushed forward with policies that weaken legacy environmental protections beneficial to birds — such as Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Endangered Species Act and National Environmental Protection Act. At the same time, thousands of federal conservation workers were laid off.

Bird Conservancy was all too familiar with the Trump administration's attacks on climate change and environmental protection. In the last three months of 2025, the organization confronted the termination of a $1.3 million Department of the Interior grant and the freezing of another significant portion of funds due to the 43-day government shutdown. The cuts amounted to roughly 10% of the organization’s annual budget.

“The justification for these cuts isn’t totally clear, but the message was unmistakable: our commitment to a vision where everyone has access to and feels welcome in nature has somehow become controversial,” Bird Conservancy executive director Tammy VerCouteren and communications director Jacob Job wrote in Primary Source, the organization’s bi-annual magazine.  

The majority of the lost funding was for a program called the Sustainable Grazing Network Program. Under this program, Bird Conservancy works with private landowners in Mexico on long-term conservation projects on their properties, in order to restore and maintain grassland habitat in the non-breeding grounds, while also conducting grassland bird monitoring.

The other programs that endured cuts included a database called the Avian Conservation Assessment Database — a tool used to inform conservation decision making at all kinds of different jurisdictional levels — and the Integrated Monitoring in Bird Conservation Regions Program, the second largest breeding bird monitoring program in North America created to address large-scale declines in avian populations.

But despite these hardships, Bird Conservancy weathered the storm and its mission has remained unchanged. While much of the staff — including Reese and Ryder — had to work on three-quarters time during the shutdown, the organization managed to avoid layoffs, retained their programs and successfully incurred its largest fundraising campaign ever last year.

“It's a big challenge to try to do work as a bird conservation organization, to try to reverse or even stall bird population declines,” Reese said. “It's hard to get enough funding to do the research and to do the conservation work that we want to and there's a lot of competing land uses that we're not going to be able to change.”

“But one of the really cool things about the work that Bird Conservancy of the Rockies does, especially surrounding our work with grassland bird conservation, is we work really closely with a lot of private ranchers across the Great Plains,” she continued.

*******

‘What’s good for the bird is good for the herd.’

While ranching and farming have contributed to the loss of grasslands, it is also a crucial part of the solution moving forward.

Across the Great Plains, several conservation organizations are working with ranchers and farmers to implement management strategies that help birds and the land. Though once at odds, thousands of ranchers and farmers have assisted in the maintenance and restoration of healthy grasslands.  

Sarah Sortum — the fourth generation co-owner of Switzer Ranch, in Loup County, Nebraska — began implementing bird conservation practices on her land about 15 years ago. The spread of red cedar had become a big issue for her family’s cattle operations and for their bird watching tourism business.

To address this, Sortum mechanically removed the trees followed by a period of prescribed burning, in an effort to restore and maintain the birds’ prairie habitat.

“I really see the birds on our place as my barometer for our overall resource health,” she said. “I mean, can you imagine a world without birds? Come on. They add a quality to life that just makes everything more special.”
