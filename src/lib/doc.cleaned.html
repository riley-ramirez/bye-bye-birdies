A Berkeley Journalism School analysis of the North American Breeding Bird Survey and Partners in Flight data shows that the chestnut-collared longspur population has lost around 9 million birds in the last 50 years, and could lose another 2 million more in the next 50 years.

Significant degradation and loss of grassland habitat has caused this species to disappear from much of its historical range.

In an effort to reverse — or at least stall — the wide scale loss of the chestnut-collared longspur and several other grassland birds, scientists have turned to a tiny tracking device that provides answers to aid conservation.

The sun has barely risen on a below freezing, cloudless January day when research scientist Jessie Reese and the researchers from Bird Conservancy of the Rockies pull off onto a dirt track in the Chihuahuan Desert.  

It’s their ninth consecutive day of searching for key grassland bird species facing significant population declines.

The team of biologists followed these tiny songbirds to their New Mexico wintering grounds in order to study the threats they are facing, as over the last 50 years, each species has lost between 60 to 70 percent of their populations.

As they near their worksite, Reese and avian ecologists, Nick Haddad and Kylie Lamoree, poke their heads out of the truck windows, carefully scanning the golden and dusty-green grasses for chestnut-collared longspurs, horned larks, grasshopper sparrows and baird's sparrows.

Reese started out studying birds in a similar fashion — with her head out of a car window, more than 15 years ago.

She became interested in researching birds after taking a class at James Madison University where her professor would load students up into a 12-passenger van at 7 a.m. and drive around the county looking at birds.

"My eyes were open to an entire world that I had no idea existed," Reese said.

But that world is currently threatened as across North America, grasslands and the birds that depend on them are rapidly vanishing, data shows.

These ground-dwelling birds — often distinguished by muted colors such as brown, white or gray to help them blend in with the landscape — are vital to the health and sustainability of the Great Plains, providing essential ecological services such as pest control, seed dispersal, nutrient cycling and pollination.

In the last half-century, grassland birds have lost over half of their populations, declining over 40 percent. And despite ongoing efforts by conservationists and landowners across the Great Plains to restore habitat and curb losses, the declines are accelerating, according to a new study in the journal Science. Those declines are exacerbated by climate change and cuts to laws and governmental funding that protect birds and their habitats.

The decrease in birds coincides with a dramatic change in open grasslands, which is being converted into farmland at a rate of TK acres per year, resulting in TK acres loss since 1985. The collapse of the birds and grasslands could result in a modern day Dust Bowl, which scientists say would cause major disruptions in the U.S. food supply and economy.  

Scientists hope their research coupled with new programs will curb the losses and result in a rebound in some of the birds lost, because anything else could be catastrophic.

“Are we going into an extinction crisis? Well, if we don’t have conservation professionals and we don’t invest in it, then yeah, we likely will,” Brandt Ryder, chief conservation scientist at Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, said.

Reese, who has led Bird Conservancy’s Migratory Ecology Program since January 2025, is one of many conservation professionals racing to figure out how to stall this looming extinction crisis.

Started in 2020, the ecology program is an initiative aimed at increasing the body of knowledge around grassland bird migration across the Great Plains, but especially in the Chihuahuan Desert, which has been relatively understudied.

For the past three winters, field crews from Bird Conservancy have traveled to New Mexico to tag and monitor grassland birds on their non-breeding grounds using Motus technology.

The Motus Wildlife Tracking System, started in 2014 by Birds Canada, is a global collaborative network of automated telemetry stations that allows researchers to study the movements of small animals tagged with radio transmitters. Prior to 2020, there was a significant lack of Motus stations in the Great Plains and longstanding research on grassland birds had mostly been conducted on the breeding grounds.

“Bird Conservancy has really helped the Motus Network grow in the Great Plains and the Chihuahuan Desert,” Reese said. In the past five years, the organization has installed over 100 radio towers and tagged almost 400 grassland birds.

A biome in collapse

Despite being among the most endangered ecosystems in the U.S., grasslands are the least protected, according to TK? What are the other ecosystems and how are they protected?

“Grasslands are the lifeblood of the central part of the U.S. and if they disappear, then this landscape will be drastically altered,” Ryder said.

According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 62 percent of the North American grassland biome has been lost due to agricultural conversion, woody-plant invasion, drought and other climate change-related stressors. World Wildlife Fund estimates that 1 to 2 million acres of grasslands continue to be lost each year.

The loss of this land would reduce carbon sequestration, diminish a threatened pollinator habitat and reduce soil quality and moisture retention.

As climate change triggers rising temperatures, shifts in precipitation patterns and more frequent extreme weather events such as drought and wildfires, the loss of healthy grasslands for birds and agriculture falls into an intensifying spiral.

Contributing to the collapse is the country’s need for crops and beef. Roughly one-fourth of the county’s beef is grown in the Great Plains, creating a tension between birds and agriculture.

In the Southern and Central Great Plain states, another threat driving the loss of grasslands includes the encroachment of woody plants, such as red cedar and mesquite trees, has taken over the landscape.

Throughout history, indigenous land practices and free roaming bison helped to maintain the grassland biome. However, after decades of fire suppression, the near elimination of bison on the land, as well as the creation of windbreaks for agricultural fields, the trees have grown rampant.

“The reality is that we can't restore all of the grassland that's been lost in North America,” Reese said. “So we really need to understand where we should be doing conservation work — are there places that are going to be more beneficial where we'll get a bigger bang for our conservation buck?”
