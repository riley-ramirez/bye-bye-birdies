DEK: Six years after scientists reported that billions of birds vanished from the North American landscape since 1970, conservationists are still racing to reverse catastrophic losses that show no sign of slowing down.

The sun has barely risen on the below freezing, cloudless January day when research scientist Jessie Reese and the researchers from Bird Conservancy of the Rockies pull off onto a dirt track in the Chihuahuan Desert.  

It’s their ninth consecutive day of searching for key grassland bird species facing significant declines in their populations.

The team of biologists followed these tiny songbirds to their New Mexico wintering grounds in order to study the threats they are facing, as over the last 50 years, each species has lost between 60 to 70 percent of their populations.

As they near their worksite, Reese and avian ecologists, Nick Haddad and Kylie Lamoree, poke their heads out of the truck windows, carefully scanning the golden and dusty-green grasses for Chestnut-collared Longspurs, Horned Larks, Grasshopper Sparrows and Baird’s Sparrows.

Reese started out studying birds in a similar fashion — with her head out of a car window, more than 15 years ago.

She became interested in researching birds after taking a class at James Madison University where her professor would load students up into a 12-passenger van at 7 a.m. and drive around the county looking at birds.

"My eyes were open to an entire world that I had no idea existed," Reese said.

But that world is currently being threatened as across North America, grasslands and the birds that depend on them are rapidly vanishing, data shows.

These ground-dwelling birds — often distinguished by muted colors such as brown, white or gray to help them blend in with the landscape — are vital to the health and sustainability of the Great Plains, providing essential ecological services such as pest control, seed dispersal, nutrient cycling and pollination.

In the last half-century, grassland birds have lost over half of their populations, declining at a rate of over 40 percent. And despite ongoing efforts by conservationists and landowners across the Great Plains to restore habitat and curb losses, the declines are accelerating, according to a new study in the journal Science. Those declines are exacerbated by climate change and cuts to laws that protect birds and their habitats.

Scientists hope their research coupled with new programs will curb the losses and result in a rebound in some of the birds lost, because anything else could be catastrophic.

“Are we going into an extinction crisis? Well, if we don’t have conservation professionals and we don’t invest in it, then yeah, we likely will,” Brandt Ryder, chief conservation scientist at Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, said.

Crew leader Sophie Barno and George Kinney — who could both identify nearly every type of grass by name — had been to this exact site the previous year. They noticed stark differences in the diversity of grasses and birds present. The change, they believe, was largely due to heavy rainfall in the state last summer.

A biome in collapse

Despite being among the most endangered ecosystems in the U.S., grasslands are the least protected.

While some grassland birds are generalists and able to use a variety of different habitats, obligate species strictly require large, uninterrupted and tree-less landscapes throughout the breeding, non-breeding and migratory seasons. The topography of the land is especially important for breeding, as many grassland birds build their nests on the ground, relying on dense grass to cover and protect their eggs and young from predators.

Of the 24 obligate species outlined in the 2025 State of the Birds Report, 13 are in steep decline. These birds are highly sensitive to changes in the environment.

According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 62 percent of the North American grassland biome has been lost due to agricultural conversion, woody-plant invasion, drought and other climate change-related stressors. World Wildlife Fund estimates that 1 to 2 million acres of grasslands continue to be lost each year.

The Great Plains ecosystem is as important for birds as it is for the entire United States’ population.

Roughly one-fourth of all U.S. crops and half of the country’s beef cattle are grown in the Great Plains. Additionally, healthy grasslands provide vital ecosystem services such as carbon sequestration, pollinator habitat, improved soil quality and moisture retention.

As climate change triggers rising temperatures, shifts in precipitation patterns and more frequent extreme weather events such as drought and wildfires, the loss of healthy grasslands for birds and agriculture falls into an intensifying spiral.

“Grasslands are the lifeblood of the central part of the U.S. and if they disappear, then this landscape will be drastically altered,” Ryder said.

In the Southern and Central Great Plain states, another threat is driving the loss of grasslands. Over the past few decades, the encroachment of woody plants, such as red cedar and mesquite trees, has taken over the landscape.

Throughout history, indigenous land practices and free roaming bison helped to maintain the grassland biome. However, after decades of fire suppression, the near elimination of bison on the land, as well as the creation of windbreaks for agricultural fields, the trees have grown rampant.

“The reality is that we can't restore all of the grassland that's been lost in North America,” Reese said. “So we really need to understand where we should be doing conservation work — are there places that are going to be more beneficial where we'll get a bigger bang for our conservation buck?”

A recent trend in duck populations might indicate an even more concerning future for grassland birds.

Through its models created using data from Motus and various other sources, one of the places Bird Conservancy has determined as a high conservation priority is the Prairie Pothole Region of the Northern Great Plains.

The Prairie Pothole Region consists of a mixture of depressional wetlands and prairie habitat that are used as breeding grounds for up to 50 percent of North America’s duck and waterfowl populations, as well as several grassland species.

In contrast to grassland species, wetland and waterfowl populations have been on an upward trend since 1970, as a result of successful conservation movements and environmental policies like the Clean Air and Water Acts, Farm Bill and North American Waterfowl Management Plan.  

However, after hitting record high levels around 2016, duck populations have been in a fairly steady decline due to severe, widespread drought across the prairie potholes.

Michael Brasher, senior waterfowl scientist for Ducks Unlimited, said that this is not entirely abnormal. The fluctuations in duck populations have been historically tied to the health of the area, which is naturally subjected to periodic drought.

At the same time, the Prairie Pothole Region — like the rest of the Great Plains — is shrinking. The Environmental Protection Agency estimates that up to 60 percent of the Prairie Pothole wetlands have been altered, drained or destroyed due to increased agricultural and commercial development.

Data shows that some grassland birds — including the Chestnut-collared Longspur, Baird’s Sparrow, Lark Bunting, Thick-billed Longspur and Sprague’s Pipit — are declining nearly twice as fast in the Prairie Pothole Region as compared to the rest of the United States.

In addition, the Trump administration has pushed forward weakening legacy environmental protection laws and policies beneficial to birds — such as Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, Endangered Species Act and National Environmental Protection Act — and hundreds of government conservation workers have been laid off. All of these threats to birds are compounded by climate change, which is expected to increase the frequency and severity of drought.

So while duck populations are eventually expected to increase again, conservationists worry that the species won’t be able to rebound to their former levels.

“Despite still being a conservation success story, we have probably more risks now to duck habitats and duck populations than we have had in a long time,” Brasher said.

*******

About 60 to 70 percent of Bird Conservancy’s funding comes through federal sources, according to Ryder.

Last year, the organization felt the impacts of the Trump administration attacks on climate change and the environment through the termination of a $1.3 million Department of the Interior grant, the freezing of another significant portion of funds and the 43-day government shutdown.

“The justification for these cuts isn’t totally clear, but the message was unmistakable: our commitment to a vision where everyone has access to and feels welcome in nature has somehow become controversial,” Bird Conservancy executive director Tammy VerCouteren and communications director Jacob Job wrote in Primary Source, the organization’s bi-annual magazine.  

The majority of the lost funding was for a program called the Sustainable Grazing Network Program. Under this program, Bird Conservancy works with private landowners down in Mexico on long term conservation projects on their properties, in order to restore and maintain grassland habitat in the non-breeding grounds. The organization also conducts grassland bird monitoring on those properties.

The other programs that endured cuts included a database called the Avian Conservation Assessment Database — a tool used to inform conservation decision making at all kinds of different jurisdictional levels — and the Integrated Monitoring in Bird Conservation Regions Program, the second largest breeding bird monitoring program in North America created to address large-scale declines in avian populations.

But despite these hardships, Bird Conservancy weathered the storm and its mission has remained unchanged. While much of the staff had to work on three-quarters time during the shutdown, the organization managed to avoid layoffs and successfully incurred its largest fundraising campaign ever last year.

“It's a big challenge to try to do work as a bird conservation organization, to try to reverse or even stall bird population declines,” Reese said. “It's hard to get enough funding to do the research and to do the conservation work that we want to and there's a lot of competing land uses that we're not going to be able to change. But one of the really cool things about the work that Bird Conservancy of the Rockies does, especially surrounding our work with grassland bird conservation, is we work really closely with a lot of private ranchers across the Great Plains.”

‘What’s good for the bird is good for the herd.’

While ranching and farming have contributed to the loss of grasslands, it is also a crucial part of the solution moving forward.

Across the Great Plains, Bird Conservancy and several other conservation organizations are working with ranchers and farmers to implement management strategies that help birds and the land.

Through programs like Bird Conservancy’s Sustainable Grazing Network, Audubon’s Conservation Ranching Program and the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s Conservation Reserve Program, thousands of ranchers and farmers have assisted in the maintenance and restoration of healthy grasslands.  

“There are a bunch of different flavors of programs that highlight these conservation successes,” Ryder said. “Whether Audubon Conservation Ranching or Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, we’re out there working side by side with the ranching community to help them and help us at the same time.”

Sarah Sortum — the fourth generation co-owner of Switzer Ranch, in Loup County, Nebraska — began implementing bird conservation practices on her land about 15 years ago. The spread of red cedar had become a big issue for her family’s cattle operations and for their bird watching tourism business.

To address this, Sortum mechanically removed the trees followed by a period of prescribed burning, in an effort to restore and maintain the birds’ prairie habitat.

“I really see the birds on our place as my barometer for our overall resource health,” she said. “For ​​farmers and ranchers — like any other family business — your hope and dream is that generations after you will benefit from what you've done and will be able to continue with the same opportunities. If we don't have a healthy resource, then that's going to be very challenging.”

After a while, Sortum started to notice that improving the health of the ecosystem didn’t only benefit the birds, but it also benefited their cattle operation by increasing the space and forage available for grazing.

Because of this, implementing bird conservation practices on the land is a no brainer to Sortum.

“I mean, can you imagine a world without birds? Come on. They add a quality to life that just makes everything more special,” she said.

A recent trend in duck populations might indicate an even more concerning future for grassland birds, as Bird Conservancy models created using data from Motus and various other sources reveal the Prairie Pothole Region to be of critical importance for fading species such as the Baird’s Sparrow.

The Prairie Pothole Region consists of a mixture of depressional wetlands and prairie habitat that are used as breeding grounds for up to 50 percent of North America’s duck and waterfowl populations, as well as several grassland species.

In contrast to grassland birds, wetland and waterfowl species populations have been on an upward trend since 1970, as a result of successful conservation movements and environmental policies like the Clean Air and Water Acts, Farm Bill and North American Waterfowl Management Plan.  

However, after hitting record high levels around 2016, duck populations have been in a fairly steady decline due to severe, widespread drought across the prairie potholes.

Michael Brasher, senior waterfowl scientist for Ducks Unlimited, said that this is not entirely abnormal. The fluctuations in duck populations are closely tied to the health of the area, which is naturally subjected to periodic drought.

However, at the same time, the Prairie Pothole Region — like the rest of the Great Plains — is shrinking. It is currently estimated that up to 60 percent of the Prairie Pothole wetlands have been altered, drained or destroyed due to increased agricultural and commercial development.

In addition, the Trump administration has pushed forward weakening legacy environmental protection laws and policies — such as Clean Water, Clean Air, Endangered Species and National Environmental Protection acts — and climate change is expected to increase the frequency and severity of drought.

So while duck populations are eventually expected to increase again, conservationists worry that the species won’t be able to rebound to their former levels.

“Despite still being a conservation success story, we have probably more risks now to duck habitats and duck populations than we have had in a long time,” Brasher said.

For decades, biologists from Bird Conservancy have migrated with grassland birds down to New Mexico during the wintering seasons since 1996, in order to conduct bird surveys and gather habitat data. Despite this, still little was known about specific migratory patterns and behaviors of grassland birds, causing roadblocks for conservationists when attempting to implement successful conservation practices.  

In 2020, the organization launched its migratory ecology program, working with the Motus program to track birds across their full annual cycle in the Great Plains and Chihuahuan Desert regions.  

The Motus Wildlife Tracking System is a global collaborative network of automated radio telemetry stations that allows researchers to study the movements of small animals tagged with radio transmitters. After a bird is tagged, station antennas will detect and record when the bird passes by, uploading that detection to an online database thereafter.

The network is made up of over 1,000 stations operated by nearly 900 different organizations across the United States and Canada, allowing for highly collaborative research and data sharing.

Prior to 2020, there was a large lack of Motus stations in the Great Plains region. Researchers knew little about grassland bird long distance movements, migratory timing, stopover locations or survival along the Central Flyway. This knowledge gap posed problems for conservation organizations when trying to create effective conservation strategies and practices for declining species.

“Bird Conservancy has really helped the Motus Network grow in the Great Plains and the Chihuahuan Desert,” Reese said. In the past five years, the organization has installed over 100 radio towers and tagged almost 400 grassland birds.

In doing so, the organization has begun to address that knowledge gap and identify crucial locations for conservation.

Data/Science

Over the past 50 years, scientists from U.S. bird conservation groups have been noticing an alarming trend: birds are rapidly vanishing across every North American biome. In 2019, a landmark study revealed that 3 billion breeding birds had been lost since 1970.

The findings set off a state of emergency throughout the conservation world, as scientists, policy makers and bird lovers started racing to figure out how to slow down an impending extinction crisis.

In the Great Plains, birds were disappearing at a rate higher than species in any other range, having lost more than half of their populations in the last 50 years.  

Six years later, the same remains true. Despite several efforts to curb losses, grassland birds continue to be the most imperiled species in North America, declining at a rate of 43 percent with some species like the Mountain Plover, Chestnut-collared Longspur and Baird’s Sparrow declining over 67 percent according to the 2025 State of the Birds report.

The main driver behind the widespread decline of birds in the heartland of America is habitat loss.

Despite being among the most endangered ecosystems in the U.S., grasslands are the least protected. According to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 62 percent of the North American grassland biome has been lost as a result of agricultural conversion, development and the spread of invasive trees — resulting in hundreds of millions of acres of habitat loss for birds.

It is estimated that the Great Plains region loses 1 to 2 million acres of grasslands each year.

A significant majority of birds that rely solely on grasslands for breeding are migratory, traveling long distances between their breeding and wintering grounds each year. Grasslands continue to shrink not only in places critical for breeding, but also in places needed to rest and refuel during migration and wintering months, further exacerbating bird decline.  

“The reality is that we can't restore all of the grassland that's been lost in North America. We have to eat. The Great Plains is really the breadbasket of North America,” Reese said. “We really need to understand where we should be doing conservation work — are there places that are going to be more beneficial where we'll get a bigger bang for our conservation buck?”

Habitat loss is not the only pressure impacting grassland bird populations. Climate change, pesticide usage and woody plant encroachment have also contributed to widespread declines. However, behind all of these threats is one common denominator: humans.

Importance

Without major intervention, several grassland bird species are on track to go extinct [by the end of the century].

Catastrophic losses in bird populations would dramatically alter and threaten the health of the Great Plains region, as birds provide vital ecological services to grasslands, such as pest control and seed dispersal.

As climate change triggers rising temperatures, shifts in precipitation patterns and more frequent extreme weather events such as drought and wildfires, loss of both birds and grasslands will continue to accelerate.

The implications of grassland loss extends further than just birds. With one-fourth of all U.S. crops produced in the Great Plains, declines in grassland health could mean large-scale disruptions in the country’s food supply and economy.

“Grasslands are the lifeblood of the central part of the U.S. and if they disappear, then this landscape will be drastically altered.” Brandt Ryder, chief conservation scientist at Bird Conservancy of the Rockies, said. “You can think of it like a big, giant Dust Bowl.”

Who Cares?

Restoring bird populations has wide ranging benefits that go beyond simply improving habitat health and the birds themselves. Birding is a big business.

According to the 2022 National Survey of Fishing, Hunting and Wildlife-Associated Recreation, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service estimated that bird-related activities generated almost $280 billion to the American economy and created 1.4 million jobs.

The survey also estimated that nearly 100 million Americans are bird watchers.

Chris Chappell became interested in birds at just 8 years old after he took care of a couple of chicks that fell out of a nest in his backyard.

A few years later, on a vacation with his family in Arizona, Chappell went on a field trip to a nearby wildlife refuge led by a wildlife biologist.

“We saw all these amazing birds,” Chappell said. “I still remember the American Avocet and the Black-necked stilt — looking at them through the scope. That was the moment I went from being a bird watcher, you might say, to being a birder.”

At age 15, he started leading Audubon field trips and Christmas Bird Counts in Washington state.

The National Audubon Society’s Christmas Bird Count started in 1900 and is the county’s longest running community science bird project. Since its mere beginnings over a century ago, thousands of volunteers across North and South America contribute to this wildlife census each year.

The Christmas Bird Count has enabled researchers and conservationists to study the long-term health and population trends of birds across the continent. Combined with other surveys such as the U.S. Geological Survey’s North American Breeding Bird Survey, started in 1966, the data has been vital for informing conservation strategies.

In 2002, the Cornell Lab of Ornithology launched its own participatory science program known as eBird. Since then, bird watchers worldwide have contributed to billions of bird observations that are compiled into a comprehensive database.  

Like the Christmas Bird Count, eBird has become an invaluable resource for conservation, helping scientists in better understanding bird abundance and distribution.

Without birders and hunters collecting data, many of the scientific discoveries in the bird world in the last half century might have been missed, including the documentation of widescale losses in North American bird populations.

Chappell — who is now 65-years-old and running his own bird watching tour business in Santa Fe, New Mexico — still regularly participates in Audubon’s Christmas Bird Count and is also recognized in the top 50 eBirders in the state.
