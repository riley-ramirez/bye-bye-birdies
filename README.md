# UC Berkeley Journalism Master's Multimedia Capstone Project - May 2026

<strong>Summary</strong>

Since 1970, over 3 billion birds have vanished from the North American landscape. And in the Great Plains, birds are disappearing at a rate higher than species in any other range. More than half of the birds dependent on grasslands are in steep decline, and some birds have lost up to 90% of their populations. The decrease in birds coincides with the loss of millions of acres of native grasslands in the Great Plains region. This project, “Bye Bye Birdies,” follows the story of a group of scientists who, despite a seemingly endless uphill climb, are working tirelessly to save grassland birds and their habitat. 

For this story, I interviewed several biologists and conservation scientists to understand the state of grassland birds in North America. I conducted a data analysis using R and QGIS of the North American Breeding Bird Survey, Partners in Flight data, and the Annual National Land Cover Change dataset. The analysis revealed steep declines in grassland bird populations since 1970, including the losses of millions of birds, and the decline of grassland habitat across the country. This project also incorporates video I filmed and edited of researchers in New Mexico’s Chihuahuan Desert grasslands who were monitoring and tagging key species of concern, using tiny radio tracking devices. I built several custom interactive components into this story, including a custom animated video used to highlight a key grassland bird facing significant declines in its population and a scrollable video breakdown of the monitoring process. 

Every media element lives on a custom-built website I coded by using Svelte, a coding framework that uses HTML, CSS and Javascript. Together with the written narrative, hand-drawn illustrations, original videos and photos, animations and interactive elements, this project tells the story about the state of grassland birds, but also about the resilience of a group of people who, even when faced with little hope, are still fighting to save them.

<strong>Methodology</strong>

UC Berkeley Graduate School of Journalism analyzed the U.S. Geological Survey’s North American Breeding Bird Survey core indices data from 1970-2024 to look at population trends over time for 25 grassland bird species. The selection of those species were based on the obligate grassland birds listed in the 2025 State of the Birds report. The school cleaned and analyzed the Breeding Bird Survey data using R. Population size estimates from the Partners in Flight database were then used to determine future and past population losses based on trends found from the BBS analysis. 

The analysis found declines in populations for 20 out of 25 species. Of the 20 species facing declines, 13 have declined more than 50 percent since 1970. Some species have declined nearly 90 percent. 

The school also analyzed USGS Annual National Land Cover Change data, which is generated from satellite imagery, to assess changes in grassland habitat from 1985-2024. The data was used to compare grassland land cover over time and assess grassland conversion to agricultural use. To do this, the school looked at conversion from grassland to crops and pasture, and from crops and pasture to grassland for the entire country. The data was rendered in QGIS and analyzed in R. 

Findings and methods were verified in interviews with experts at USGS.
